How Much Should I Pay Per TB for a NAS Drive?

In 2026, CMR NAS drives (IronWolf, WD Red Plus) typically cost $15–$22/TB for popular 4TB–8TB capacities. Larger drives (12TB–18TB) often reach $13–$18/TB as capacity scales. Enterprise CMR drives (Exos, WD Gold) can hit $13–$16/TB but sold by third-party vendors carry OEM warranty risk. Budget desktop CMR (WD Blue 8TB, Barracuda 8TB) reaches $10–$14/TB but lacks NAS firmware and rated for lower duty cycles.

SMR vs CMR: The Hidden Price Trap

SMR drives appear 20–35% cheaper per TB. The WD Red (SMR) 4TB at $69 vs WD Red Plus (CMR) 4TB at $79 looks like a $10 saving. But SMR in a NAS causes RAID rebuild times to jump from hours to days, with a high probability of rebuild failure on arrays larger than 4TB. One failed rebuild can cost you all your data. The "savings" evaporate against the cost of data recovery.

⚠️ CMR vs SMR: The Difference That Can Cost You Everything

Amazon listings rarely disclose CMR or SMR. Many sellers actively hide it. This section explains what it means and why it matters more than any other spec for NAS and RAID use.

🟢 CMR — Conventional Magnetic Recording

Each track is written independently. Overwriting data on any track does not affect neighboring tracks. This makes CMR drives fast on random writes, reliable during RAID rebuilds, and safe for 24/7 operation. All professional NAS drives — WD Red Plus, IronWolf, WD Gold, Seagate Exos, Toshiba N300 — use CMR.

RAID-6 rebuild on 8×8TB CMR array: ~18 hours → data safe throughout

⚠️ SMR — Shingled Magnetic Recording

Tracks overlap like roof shingles to pack more data into the same area. Overwriting requires rewriting adjacent tracks in zones — creating a "write pause" that can last seconds to minutes under sustained load. Synology, QNAP, and TrueNAS publish SMR blocklists. These drives work fine for cold storage or PC secondary drives.

⚠️ WD Red (without "Plus") is SMR. The naming is intentionally confusing. Check every drive on this tracker before buying for NAS use.

💨 Helium vs Air

Drives above 10–14TB seal helium inside instead of air. Less resistance = more platters, lower power (5–8W vs 10–14W), less vibration, and lower temperature. All Seagate Exos X-series and WD Gold above 10TB are helium-filled. This tracker marks helium drives explicitly.

📊 RPM Guide

7200 RPM: faster sequential reads/writes (150–220 MB/s), faster RAID rebuilds, louder and warmer. Best for Plex, ZFS, simultaneous multi-user NAS. 5400 RPM: quieter (20–24 dB), cooler, 30–40% less power. Fine for home NAS with 1–2 simultaneous users. 5900 RPM: Seagate's compromise speed found on IronWolf mid-capacities.

Internal Hard Drive FAQ

Everything you need to know before buying a NAS or internal HDD.

What is the difference between CMR and SMR hard drives?

CMR writes each track independently. SMR overlaps tracks for density but is slow on random writes and causes RAID rebuild failures. Always choose CMR for NAS, RAID, or any write-heavy workload. SMR is fine for cold storage and desktop secondary drives.

Are SMR hard drives bad for NAS?

Yes. Synology, QNAP, and TrueNAS explicitly warn against or block SMR drives in their NAS systems. Under the sustained write pressure of a RAID rebuild, SMR drives frequently time out and cause the rebuild to fail — potentially resulting in data loss. The cost saving over CMR is not worth the risk.

What is a good price per TB for a NAS hard drive?

In 2026: $15–$22/TB for 4TB–8TB CMR NAS drives (IronWolf, WD Red Plus). $13–$18/TB for 12TB–18TB CMR NAS drives. Enterprise CMR (Exos, WD Gold) from Amazon Retail often matches or beats NAS drives on $/TB for capacities above 10TB. Under $10/TB = usually SMR or Renewed — check this tracker.

WD Red Plus vs Seagate IronWolf — which should I buy?

Both are CMR, NAS-rated, and excellent. WD Red Plus: quieter at 5400 RPM (4TB–6TB), slightly lower power, compatible with every NAS. IronWolf: AgileArray vibration compensation is better in 8-bay+ enclosures, IronWolf Health Management works natively in Synology DSM. Tiebreaker: current $/TB on this tracker. They swap positions weekly.

Is 5400 RPM or 7200 RPM better for NAS?

7200 RPM for large arrays (8TB+ RAID, Plex with 4K transcoding, multi-user access), ZFS RAID rebuilds, and enterprise workloads. 5400 RPM for home NAS with 1–4 drives, Plex 1080p streaming, family photo storage — quieter and 40% less power. The IronWolf 8TB at 7200 RPM and WD Red Plus 8TB at 5400 RPM both perform well in typical home NAS scenarios.

What happens to warranty when buying from third-party Amazon sellers?

Drives sold by third-party sellers (not "Ships from and sold by Amazon.com") are often OEM units. OEM drives were sold to system integrators, not to consumers. WD and Seagate may refuse warranty claims on OEM drives. Always prefer "Sold by Amazon" for retail warranty. This tracker flags condition (Amazon Retail vs Third-Party) on every listing.

Are helium hard drives more reliable?

Helium drives run cooler, generate less vibration, and consume less power — all of which positively correlate with reliability in multi-drive bays. Studies from Backblaze show helium-filled drives have comparable or better annualized failure rates versus air-filled equivalents. The sealed enclosure also prevents contamination. All high-capacity enterprise drives (12TB+) use helium.

Should I buy Amazon Renewed hard drives for NAS?

Not as your primary NAS drives. Renewed drives offer 90-day Amazon warranty and unknown prior workload. Enterprise Renewed (Exos 12TB+) can be excellent for cold-storage expansion bays where occasional drive failure is acceptable — the $/TB is hard to beat. For primary data: buy new CMR from Amazon Retail with 3yr or 5yr warranty.

How many TB do I need for a home NAS?

Rule of thumb: your raw storage need × 1.5 to account for RAID redundancy. A 2-bay NAS in RAID-1 (mirrored) with 2×8TB gives 8TB usable. A 4-bay in RAID-5 with 4×8TB gives 24TB usable. For Plex with 4K content: allow 50–100GB per movie. For family photos/videos: 1–2TB/year growing. Start with 2×8TB CMR and expand as needed.
